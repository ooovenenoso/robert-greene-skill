# Law 1 - Never Outshine the Master

> **Principle:** Make your superior shine. Your light depends on theirs.

## Key phrase
"Make your boss shine; your light depends on theirs."

## Mechanics
Power is hierarchical. When a subordinate becomes more visible or talented than their superior, a primitive threat response activates. The boss needs to feel secure in their position; a subordinate who shines too brightly becomes a **danger**, not an asset.

## Practical application
- In meetings: **direct credit upward**, without false modesty
- If you have a better idea than your boss's: present it as a **refinement** of theirs, not a replacement
- In public: **praise the superior first**, speak later
- Make your boss **feel indispensable** and you will be seen as a valuable extension

## Trap
Do not be falsely submissive - superiors detect obvious flattery. If your superior is chronically weak/incompetent, this law condemns you. Consider moving. Do not outshine **everyone**: matching peers is healthy competition; outshining the boss is suicide.

## Historical case
**Nicolas Fouquet** (1661) - Superintendent of Finances for Louis XIV organized a party so lavish it eclipsed the Sun King. Louis, humiliated, arrested him three weeks later. Fouquet died in prison. The lesson: never compete in extravagance with whoever holds the monopoly on visible power.

# Law 2 - Never Put Too Much Trust in Friends, Learn How to Use Enemies

> **Principle:** Show strategic courtesy, never naivety. Extreme friendliness is interpreted as weakness.

## Key phrase
"Be more dangerous than you seem - but never seem it."

## Mechanics
Fear is a more stable motivator than love. People forget favors but not remembered threats. A total **kindness** image triggers two equally damaging responses:
1. They see you as **easy prey** -> exploit you
2. They see you as **foolish** -> despise you

The social golden rule: courtesy on the surface, calculation underneath.

## Practical application
- At work: be cordial but **never reveal your full game**
- In conflicts: use a **calm but firm** tone, never pleading
- With strangers: **moderate dose of mystery** always
- To criticism: respond with **polite distance**, not sentimentality

## Trap
Do not confuse kindness with friendship. Do not fall into total cynicism: genuine warmth opens doors that fear closes. Be careful with loved ones: this law is **modulated** with family (does not apply equally).

## Historical case
**Cleopatra** before Mark Antony: alternated seduction with challenge. When he suspected infidelity, she **retreated and fasted**. Mark Antony ran to beg forgiveness. The selective "weakness" returned her power.

# Law 3 - Conceal Your Intentions

> **Principle:** Keep people in the dark about your true plans.

## Key phrase
"He who knows what you want knows how to stop you."

## Mechanics
People resist what they can see coming. Radical transparency only works when **you have so much power that resistance is useless**. While you build your position, secrecy is your best weapon. Mystery also generates **magnetism**: what is not understood is admired.

## Practical application
- In negotiations: **never reveal your maximum budget**
- In career: do not announce your next move until it is secured
- In networks: show **results and generic process**, not detailed strategy
- Use **redirection**: when they ask your plans, answer with another question

## Trap
Absolute secrecy paralyzes - you need **allies**, and allies require **some** information. Excessive opacity generates distrust long-term. Do not confuse secrecy with lying: there are things you do not say, but what you say must be true.

## Historical case
**Odysseus (Ulysses)** for 10 years concealed his identity from Penelope and the suitors under the disguise of a beggar. Only when the action was mature did he reveal who he was - and executed the suitors in a single night. Perfect plan.

# Law 4 - Always Say Less Than Necessary

> **Principle:** The words you say in excess are the ones that cost the most.

## Key phrase
"The more you say, the more foolish you seem. The less you say, the more powerful you are."

## Mechanics
Verbosity transmits anxiety to fill silences. The powerful speak little; when they speak, everyone listens. Unspoken words create **curiosity and tension**: the listener fills the gaps with what they want to believe, and that always favors you.

## Practical application
- In meetings: speak **20% of the time**, listen **80%**
- When asked: **pause before responding** (3 seconds minimum)
- Never over-correct - let the other disavow themselves
- Strategic silence is your best friend

## Trap
Light application can seem **rudeness** or arrogance. Do not confuse verbal economy with **insensitivity** (in others' painful moments, talking more is human). There are moments for long speeches: formal presentations, legal pleadings.

## Historical case
**Confucius** said: *"Words are for hiding intentions."* The great generals and diplomats of the Chinese Warring States period used silence as a weapon. **Zhuge Liang** never spoke first in council - and when he did, everyone fell silent.

# Law 5 - So Much Depends on Reputation - Guard It with Your Life

> **Principle:** Reputation is the capital of power. Protect it with everything.

## Key phrase
"One stain on reputation can erase years of work."

## Mechanics
Reputation is a **cognitive shortcut**: before knowing you, people decide based on what they have heard. A solid reputation precedes you and opens doors. A damaged reputation closes doors you did not even know existed. Building reputation takes years; destroying it, an instant.

## Practical application
- **Never** let yourself be seen losing control in public
- Before associating with someone, evaluate their reputation: it will **infect** you, good and bad
- Take care of your **public image on the internet**: what you publish today defines you tomorrow
- Small daily moral violence (delivering, thanking, being punctual) builds invisible reputation

## Trap
False reputation collapses under pressure. Do not confuse "reputation" with "fame" - the first is earned, the second is stolen. Once broken, restoring it costs 10x more than building it.

## Historical case
**Warren Buffett** built a reputation of absolute integrity over 50 years. A single phrase of his moves markets. The reputation was not created by Buffett with marketing: it was created by **dozens of difficult decisions** that no one saw.

# Law 6 - Court Attention at All Cost

> **Principle:** What is not seen does not exist. The invisible has no power.

## Key phrase
"No one can give you what they want if they do not know you exist."

## Mechanics
We live in an economy of attention. Brilliant people who do not expose themselves are surpassed by mediocre ones who do. Quality without visibility is wasted talent. It is not enough to do - you have to **be seen doing** (or have others do it for you).

## Practical application
- In your career: **publish, speak, teach**. Hidden knowledge does not pay
- In events: position yourself where the ones who matter see you
- Use **dramatic narrative**: the more story, the more attention
- If you cannot be seen by merit, become **essential** through scarcity

## Trap
Negative attention is also attention, but it is **debt**. Avoid scandal if you can. Being visible is not being noisy. **Clowns** shout; the **powerful** appear at the right moment. Be careful with narcissism: attention without substance is a house of cards.

## Historical case
**Andy Warhol** understood this like no one: *"In the future, everyone will be famous for 15 minutes."* Prediction fulfilled. The art was an excuse; what he sold was **attention to himself** converted into a brand.

# Law 7 - Get Others to Do the Work for You, But Always Take the Credit

> **Principle:** Sow without waiting for immediate harvest. Seeds watered by others yield more than your own.

## Key phrase
"Let others harvest today - you will be planting tomorrow."

## Mechanics
Others' ego is easy fuel. If you let others take credit for something you did, you accumulate **three change coins**:
1. Their gratitude (they owe you a favor)
2. Your freedom (you are not tied to the visible outcome)
3. Your mystery (who really pulls the strings?)

## Practical application
- In teams: **publish the group's work**, not yours
- If you helped someone and they took the credit: do not reclaim, **note the favor in your memory**
- In organizational politics: **distribute the spotlight** to whoever needs it - they will remember you in silence
- Be the **author behind the scenes**: the puppeteer always has more power than the puppet

## Trap
If you always do it, you will be seen as **used**. Vary the strategy. Do not be the eternal second. At some point, **take your own credit** - total invisibility is as bad as theft. Excessive generosity without reciprocity is codependency.

## Historical case
**Bismarck** managed for years an insecure Kaiser Wilhelm I. He let Wilhelm have the visible decisions and the glory; Bismarck retained real power. All of Germany believed the Kaiser ruled. The monarchy lasted 70 years.

# Law 8 - Use Absence to Increase Respect and Value - Use the Carrot, Not the Stick

> **Principle:** The power of reward is more stable than that of punishment.

## Key phrase
"Punishing makes you feared; rewarding makes you owner."

## Mechanics
The stick generates obedience through fear, but also **chronic resentment** and latent conspiracy. The carrot generates obedience through **self-interest**: the other acts because they want to, not because they fear. The punished subject awaits your downfall; the rewarded one fears losing you.

## Practical application
- In leadership: **reward in public**, correct in private
- Distribute benefits in a **visible but controlled** way - benefit scarcity maintains motivation
- Teach others to depend on your favor (not on your authority)
- Use **punishment only when inevitable** and never arbitrarily

## Trap
Rewarding without criteria creates **pleading and opportunism**. A poorly calibrated carrot generates **acquired right**: people expect constant reward. The stick is still necessary sometimes: there are people who only understand fear.

## Historical case
**Tokugawa Ieyasu** unified Japan (1603) with a brilliant policy: he pardoned enemy lords who surrendered in time and rewarded them with lands. He punished only those who rebelled **after** surrendering. 250 years of Tokugawa peace.
