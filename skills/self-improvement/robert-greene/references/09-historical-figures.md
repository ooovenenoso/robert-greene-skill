# Historical Figures Cited

> The people who populate the examples of the 48 laws. Knowing them adds context and depth.

## Antiquity

### Odysseus (Ulysses) — King of Ithaca, 9th c. BC (?)
- Law 3 (Conceal intentions): the beggar disguise for 10 years
- Archetype of the **patient strategist**

### Cicero — Roman orator and senator, 106–43 BC
- Law 19 (Know who you're dealing with): verbally attacked Mark Antony and died for it
- **Warning**: the moment matters as much as the target

### Cincinnatus — Roman dictator, 519–430 BC
- Law 22 (Use surrender tactic): voluntarily renounced power
- Model of **strategic humility**

### Cleopatra VII — Pharaoh of Egypt, 69–30 BC
- Law 2 (Never put too much trust in friends): the selective fasting before Mark Antony
- Law 24 (Play the perfect courtier): was raised as the incarnation of Isis
- Archetype of **power through conviction**

## Middle Ages and Renaissance

### Catherine de Medici — Queen of France, 1519–1589
- Law 33 (Discover each person's weakness): her mastery of court espionage
- Law 39 (Stir up emotions): used public mourning as a political weapon

### The Medici — Florentine family, 14th–18th c.
- Law 11 (Learn to keep people dependent): interdependent network with artists, popes, bankers
- Model of **soft power across centuries**

### Leonardo da Vinci — 1452–1519
- Law 15 (Crush your enemy totally): apprentice of Verrocchio
- Law 41 (Avoid stepping into a great man's shoes): worked for multiple patrons simultaneously

### Machiavelli — Political philosopher, 1469–1527
- Law 38 (Think freely, behave conventionally): his secret texts vs his public service
- Author of *The Prince*, parallel text to *48 Laws*

### Zhuge Liang — Chinese strategist, 181–234
- Law 4 (Always say less): his strategic silence in council

### Sun Tzu — Chinese general, 5th c. BC (text) / 4th c. BC (references)
- Law 17 (Control the territory): the battlefield is chosen
- Author of *The Art of War*

## Modern Era (1500–1800)

### Nicolas Fouquet — Superintendent of Finances of France, 1615–1680
- Law 1 (Never outshine the master): his party eclipsed Louis XIV
- Died in prison

### Louis XIV ("The Sun King") — King of France, 1638–1715
- Law 1: the receiver of Fouquet's error
- Model of **absolute monarchy that understood power as spectacle** (Law 37)

### Madame de Pompadour — Royal favorite, 1721–1764
- Law 12 (Selective honesty + generosity): 19 years of royal favor
- Law 8 (Carrot): converted royal favor into policy

### Talleyrand — French diplomat, 1754–1838
- Laws 10, 14, 22, 44: the perfect chameleon who survived 4 regimes
- Archetype of **political survival**

### Tokugawa Ieyasu — Japanese shogun, 1543–1616
- Law 8 (Carrot): pardoned those who surrendered, punished only late rebels
- Created 250 years of peace

### Napoleon Bonaparte — French emperor, 1769–1821
- Law 17 (Control the territory): his defeat at Waterloo for not stopping
- Law 47 (Do not go past the target): ambition without limit

### Augustus Caesar (Octavian) — Roman emperor, 63 BC–14 AD
- Law 47 (Do not go past the target): after Actium, he did not massacre Antony's children
- Created 200 years of Pax Romana

## Contemporary Era (1800–today)

### Hernán Cortés — Spanish conquistador, 1485–1547
- Law 9 (Win through actions): burned his ships
- Law 28 (Bold action): conquest of an empire with 600 men

### Andrew Carnegie — Industrialist and philanthropist, 1835–1919
- Law 40 (Despise the free lunch): gave away 90% only after obtaining it

### John D. Rockefeller — Oil magnate, 1839–1937
- Law 42 (Strike the shepherd): controlled 90% of US oil

### Mahatma Gandhi — Independence leader, 1869–1948
- Law 17 (Control the territory): the salt march changed the field
- Law 39 (Stir up emotions): non-violence as political provocation

### Charles Darwin — Naturalist, 1809–1882
- Law 35 (Master the art of timing): rigid 90-minute blocks of work

### Howard Schultz — CEO of Starbucks, b. 1953
- Law 28 (Bold action): bet on massive expansion when no one believed
- Law 27 (Cult): created the "third wave of coffee"

### Nelson Mandela — South African leader, 1918–2013
- Law 34 (Act like a king): 27 years of prison did not take his posture
- Law 47 (Do not go past the target): reconciliation instead of revenge

### J.K. Rowling — Writer, b. 1965
- Law 30 (Make accomplishment seem like effort): the confession of 12 rejections
- Law 26 (Keep your hands clean): strategic vulnerability

### Steve Jobs — Co-founder of Apple, 1955–2011
- Law 25 (Re-create yourself): firing → NeXT → Pixar → return to Apple
- Law 27 (Cult): "Think Different"
- Law 32 (Play on fantasies): didn't sell phones, sold identity
- Law 37 (Create compelling spectacles): every keynote was a cinematic event
- Law 45 (Beg for something big): "Do you want to sell sugar water all your life?"

### Bill Clinton — US President, b. 1946
- Law 26 (Keep your hands clean): the Lewinsky scandal
- Law 39 (Stir up emotions): "I feel your pain"

### Martin Luther King Jr. — Civil rights leader, 1929–1968
- Law 39 (Stir up emotions): "I have a dream" — emotion + logic
- Law 17 (Control the territory): marched where the image dominated

### Banksy — Street artist, identity unknown
- Law 16 (Use absence): 17+ years without showing his face

### Bruce Lee — Actor and martial philosopher, 1940–1973
- Law 48 (Assume formlessness): "Be water, my friend"
- Creator of Jeet Kune Do, system without system

### Barack Obama — US President, b. 1961
- Law 43 (Work on hearts and minds): combination of emotion + concrete plans

### Reed Hastings — Co-founder of Netflix, b. 1960
- Law 23 (Concentrate): DVD by mail for 7 years before streaming
- Law 28 (Bold action): bet on streaming against Blockbuster

### Warren Buffett — Investor, b. 1930

- Law 31 (Control the options): 10-15 positions held for decades

### Churchill — British prime minister, 1874–1965
- Law 39 (Stir up emotions): the WWII speeches
- Law 33 (Discover each person's weakness): understood Stalin's wound

### Abraham Lincoln — US President, 1809–1865
- Law 46 (Never appear too perfect): unkempt appearance and rustic humor
- Model of **accessible power**

## How to use this list

1. **To go deeper**: if a law resonates, search for more about the figure that embodies it
2. **For contrast**: many laws apply to the same person (e.g. Steve Jobs in 5+ laws)
3. **For historical context**: each historical case is only one example — the law applies in thousands of similar situations
